Sensor Node

A waterproof ultrasonic sensor (JSN-SR04T) is mounted on a pole above the water surface, pointing downward. It fires a 40kHz pulse, measures the echo return time, and measures the distance to the water surface. The device wakes every few minutes, takes a reading, and transmits over LoRa radio — then goes back to sleep to preserve battery. No mains power. No cellular SIM.

ESP32 microcontroller JSN-SR04T ultrasonic Solar option available
LoRaWAN Gateway

A single gateway — a small weatherproof box mounted on a rooftop or utility pole — receives transmissions from sensor nodes up to 2km away in urban areas and further in open terrain. One gateway can serve dozens of sensor nodes across an area. It connects to the internet over ethernet or WiFi and forwards readings to the server. If LoRa coverage is unavailable at a site, a mobile data plan can be used as a fallback.

~2km urban · 10km+ line of sight One gateway, many sensors No cellular required NB-IoT fallback available
Network Server

Once the gateway receives a reading from a sensor node, it passes the data to a server that decides what to do with it. This is where the alert rules live — if the water level crosses a set threshold, an alert fires. The server is privately operated, meaning Halifax Water's data stays under Halifax Water's control. Over time, readings are compared against rainfall data from Environment Canada to build a clearer picture of which culverts are most at risk and when.

Halifax Water-operated gateway The Things Stack (open source) Threshold + rate-of-rise logic Weather API integration
Alert

When a threshold is breached, a webhook fires within seconds, delivering an alert to email, SMS, or an operations dashboard. The early warning gives field crews time to inspect, close roads proactively, or deploy temporary measures. All data is logged for post-event analysis and model refinement.

Email / SMS / dashboard Configurable early warning < 2 min alert latency Configurable recipients
Connectivity Options

Why LoRaWAN, and what if it isn't available?

LoRaWAN is the preferred technology — but the sensor hardware works with multiple connectivity options. If LoRa coverage doesn't reach a specific site, we switch the radio module and keep everything else identical.

What Can Be Detected

More than just
rising water.

A single upstream sensor gives you two distinct alert types — flood early warning and blockage detection, from the same hardware.

Flood early warning
Water level rising with rainfall

During intense rainfall, upstream water backs up against the culvert as flow exceeds capacity. The sensor detects the rising surface and fires an alert before overtopping, giving operations teams time to respond before road damage occurs.

Threshold alert Rate-of-rise alert Weather correlated
Blockage detection
Water rising faster than rain explains

When debris blocks the culvert inlet, water backs up faster than rainfall alone would predict. If the sensor is mounted directly above the inlet, floating debris also reflects the ultrasonic pulse back sooner — giving a falsely high reading that triggers an alert. A blockage caught in dry weather is easy to clear. During a storm it becomes a washout.

Anomalous rate-of-rise Debris surface reflection Dry-weather trigger
Optional extension: upstream + downstream sensors

Adding a second sensor downstream allows direct flow differential measurement. If upstream is rising but downstream is not, the culvert is blocked. This gives maintenance teams a precise, actionable signal even without rainfall context.

Benefits
  • Unambiguous blockage confirmation
  • Flow rate estimation across culvert
  • Richer dataset for ML model training
  • Useful even in dry conditions
Tradeoffs
  • Two installation points per site
  • Two potential failure points to maintain
  • Downstream access can be difficult
Pilot Proposal

One site. Three months. No cost to Halifax Water.

The proposal is deliberately small — a single proof-of-concept deployment at a known problem culvert. Data is advisory only. No operational dependency.

Proof of concept

One sensor node at one candidate culvert identified by Halifax Water. Data streams to a shared dashboard. Simple threshold alerts. Hardware supplied at no cost.

  • —Sensor survives a period of exposure to Nova Scotia weather without failure
  • —Readings are consistent and credible vs. visual inspection
  • —At least one real rainfall event captured and logged
  • —Alert fires correctly when water level rises
  • —Battery life exceeds 3 months in field conditions

Multi-site expansion

Expand to 3–5 known problem sites across Halifax Water's network. Sensor data is correlated with Environment Canada rainfall, each site builds up a picture of how it behaves in different storm conditions, and anomaly detection begins to distinguish blockage from flood events. A shared dashboard gives Halifax Water real-time visibility across all monitored sites.

Deployment: ~$3,000–4,000 CAD · grant-fundable
Ongoing: platform retainer + per-node monthly fee

Predictive intelligence

Machine learning model trained on sensor + weather + historical flood data. Predictive alerts fire before water rises, based on rainfall forecast and catchment characteristics. Node deployment scales across Halifax Water's full culvert network — and potentially province-wide across NSTIR road culverts and other NS municipalities.
